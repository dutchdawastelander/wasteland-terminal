NO HOME IN THE WASTE
Chapter 3 — The Memorial

Day 22 – Settling In

We’ve stayed. The world outside doesn’t call to us anymore. The cabin’s walls keep out the wind, if not the memories.

She spends most of her time down by the creek, collecting stones. She says she’s building something. I don’t ask what. The silence between us has changed. It used to be distance. Now it feels like truce.

Day 25 – The Creek

She’s made a small circle of smooth river stones—perfect ring, every rock washed clean. Inside, one word scratched into the dirt: His.

When she caught me looking, she said, “It’s not for you to touch.” Her voice was calm, but her hands were trembling.

That night she stayed outside after dark. When she came in, she smelled like rain and smoke.

Day 27 – The Truth We Don’t Say

I dreamed of him again.

We were scavenging for food—one of the last days in New York. The air was thick with dust, the streets echoing. He was with us—our son—small, curious, clutching her hand.

He found a can under a pile of glass, held it up to show her. The light hit his face through the cracks in the ceiling. For one small second, it looked like life again.

Then came the sound—claws scraping concrete, that wet, gurgling hiss. The ghoul burst through the back door, fast. Too fast. I shouted. She turned.

It was already on him.

Her scream split the air. I fired. Missed. Fired again. The bullet went through both of them.

When I reached them, he was already still. She was holding him, rocking back and forth, whispering his name. She kept saying, He’s warm, he’s still warm.

We buried him behind a ruined church two days later. The ground was frozen. She dug with her hands until her nails tore.

Every swing of her machete since that day has been for him.

Day 29 – The Memorial

She finished it. The stones stand waist-high now, with a small wooden carving on top. She planted wildflowers around the base, pale and stubborn.

We stood together in front of it for a long time. Neither of us spoke. Then she reached for my hand.

The wind moved through the trees—soft, warm, almost kind. For a moment it felt like something inside both of us finally exhaled.

Maybe forgiveness doesn’t need words. Maybe hope doesn’t either.
