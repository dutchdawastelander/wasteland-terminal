NO HOME IN THE WASTE
Chapter 1 — The Road Out of New York

Day 1 – Leaving the City

The city didn’t fall in a single day. It just… decayed. Street by street, window by window, until it was nothing but a skeleton trying to remember what light felt like.

We left at dawn. Smoke rolled across the river, heavy and low. The Holland Tunnel was half-flooded, but the car still ran. Every time the tires hit something under the water, I told myself it was debris.

She stared out the window the whole way. Hands folded. Jaw tight. We haven’t spoken much since the loss. Silence travels with us now like a third passenger.

Outside the city, the air changed—thinner, cleaner, sharp in my lungs. For a moment it almost felt like hope, but hope doesn’t last long anymore.

Day 2 – Turnpike

The highway stretched out like an old scar. We passed collapsed bridges, rusted cars, and homes with curtains still hanging in shattered windows—ghosts of lives that stopped mid-sentence.

Every now and then she’d point at something—a billboard, a bird—and then stop herself, as if remembering it doesn’t matter anymore.

At sunset we found an overpass and built a fire beneath it. The light caught her face—she looked older than yesterday. She asked quietly, “How do we go on without him?” I didn’t have an answer.

Day 3 – Rest Stop

We camped in a burned-out rest area tonight. One wall still stood. I broke up a table for kindling.

She sat across from me, turning her ring again and again in the firelight. I kept my eyes on the flame and wrote this down:

The road only goes forward now. Whether we want it to or not.

Tomorrow we head south, toward the mountains. Toward something still alive.
